The British Museum has sought public assistance in recovering around 2,000 stolen artifacts, with a new website and email hotline launched for information sharing. The missing items, stolen over several years and valued in the millions of pounds, include gold jewelry and semi-precious gemstones dating from the 15th century BC to the 19th century AD.

The museum, collaborating with the Art Loss Register, has recovered 60 items and is in the process of retrieving 300 more. The theft has sparked renewed debates over contested artifacts, some acquired during British colonial rule, prompting calls for their return by countries like Greece and China.

The museum is cautious about sharing specific details to prevent potential exploitation, while providing images of similar items for reference.

The British Museum is facing heightened scrutiny and calls for repatriation of artifacts following the theft of around 2,000 items and accusations of mishandling. Some stolen pieces, including a Roman artifact worth Â£50,000, were reportedly sold on eBay.

The thefts are under investigation by the Metropolitan Police, and the museum's former director resigned amid revelations of unaddressed theft reports. A senior curator was fired and accused of online artifact sales. The museum, lacking a full catalog, faces criticism for not detecting losses earlier.

Various countries, including Nigeria and China, are demanding the return of specific artifacts, challenging the museum's justification for retaining them. The scandal also involves the purchase of an Egyptian artifact from a convicted smuggler, prompting ongoing investigations.

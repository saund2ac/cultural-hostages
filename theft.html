Top page

Throughout its history, Britain has grappled with the legacy of its imperial past. The nation is currently facing increasing pressure from other countries and activists to return items they claim were taken during the era of the British Empire.

From the late 16th century to the 20th century, the British Empire expanded its influence globally, establishing colonies, dominions, and protectorates, ultimately becoming the largest empire in history. While British rule brought about some aspects of modernization in the colonized nations, it also impeded self-governance, democracy, and equality under the law. According to advocates for repatriation, many cultural artifacts currently exhibited in British museums were unlawfully obtained from colonized populations.

The British Museum, housing over 8 million artifacts, including notable items like the Benin Bronzes and the Parthenon Marbles, is often cited as possessing a significant number of culturally sensitive items acquired through what activists argue was looting, as highlighted by human rights lawyer Geoffrey Robertson.

The Rosetta Stone

The Rosetta Stone, housed in the British Museum, is considered a significant artifact that played a crucial role in aiding researchers in decoding and comprehending the cultures and history of Ancient Egypt.

Originally seized from Egypt by Napoleon Bonaparte, who is widely acknowledged for introducing the country to the rest of Europe and sparking "Egyptomania" in the 19th century, the Rosetta Stone was later acquired by the British following their victory over the French in 1815.

The Rosetta Stone has become a focal point in discussions about restitution of cultural objects, although some experts are skeptical about the British Museum relinquishing one of its most renowned acquisitions.

Koh-i-Noor

Atop the opulent crown of the King of England rests the Koh-i-Noor, one of the world's largest cut diamonds.

Known as the "mountain of light," this precious gem was originally part of the Mughal Peacock Throne. After changing hands multiple times amid conflict and wars, it eventually came into the possession of Queen Victoria following the British annexation of India in 1849.

With its tumultuous history marked by numerous battles and conflicts among men, the Koh-i-Noor has garnered a superstition that deems it a jinx for men. As a result, tradition dictates that it is only passed down to the women in the British royal family.

Presently, the Koh-i-Noor diamond is showcased at the Jewel House within the Tower of London. Despite persistent claims and demands for its return by the governments of India, Pakistan, Iran, and Afghanistan since India gained independence in 1947, the British government has consistently refuted these requests. The argument put forth is that the acquisition of the gem was legally justified.

Tipu's Tiger

Tipu's Tiger is a wooden semi-automaton figure of nearly life-size depicting a soldier of the East India Company being mauled by a tiger. Operated by turning a handle, it features the soldier's arm moving up and down, accompanied by simulated dying moans produced by an organ concealed within the tiger.

Commissioned for Tipu Sultan, the ruler of the Kingdom of Mysore in the late 18th century, the tiger symbolized Tipu's main emblem, integrated into various aspects of his rule, including his throne, military equipment, soldier attire, and palace embellishments.

Following Tipu Sultan's defeat by the East India Company in 1799, British forces killed the sultan and confiscated the toy tiger along with dismantled sections of his throne. Currently, it is exhibited at the Victoria and Albert Museum, celebrated as one of its "most famous and intriguing objects," as mentioned on the museum's website.

Seeds of Hevea Brasiliensis

n 1876, British explorer Henry Wickham engaged in an act of "bio-piracy" by smuggling 70,000 seeds of Hevea brasiliensis, the Par√° rubber tree, from the Brazilian rainforests. This act played a pivotal role in the global rubber trade, impacting industries like auto tires and electrical insulation. The ensuing rubber boom from 1879 to 1912 in the Amazon Basin fueled European colonization, resulting in wealth but also causing slavery, abuse of Indigenous people, and cultural disruption. The British Empire's successful propagation of the stolen seeds led to the establishment of rubber plantations, breaking Brazil's monopoly on rubber.

The Ring of Tipu Sultan

After the British defeated Tipu Sultan, also known as the Tiger of Mysore, in 1799, they seized his sword and ring. While the sword was eventually returned to India, the ring faced a different fate. In 2014, the British auctioned the 41.2g ring, adorned with jewels and inscribed with the name of the Hindu God Ram in raised Devanagari. The ring fetched a substantial ¬£145,000, nearly ten times its estimated price, at the auction in central London, with the buyer remaining undisclosed, as per information from Christie's website.

Wine Cup of Shah Jahan

A white jade wine cup, once belonging to Shah Jahan, the emperor of the Mughal Empire famous for constructing the Taj Mahal in honor of his beloved queen, is adorned with intricate details. The cup features a lotus flower below it, acanthus leaves, and a handle shaped like an animal with a goat, horn, and beard. In the 19th century, Colonel Charles Seton Guthrie stole this exquisite wine jar, transporting it to Britain. Since 1962, the cup has been housed at the Victoria and Albert Museum in London.
